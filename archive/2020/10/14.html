@JadeMasterMath For convenience, let n be even. Each prime power in the factorization of n choose n/2 is at most n. As n choose n/2 grows super-polynomially, it follows that the number of primes grows infinite. Specifically, there are at least log_n(n choose n/2) many primes below n.

@JadeMasterMath It's not hard to show that this lower bound is within a constant factor of an upper bound as well. Thus, the number of primes below n is Θ(n/log(n)). This is a weak form of the Prime Number Theorem that can be proven simply and elementarily, to interested children even.
