@sigfpe The Fourier transform of the Gaussian f(x) = e^(-x^2) is famously just a minor reparametrization of itself. On our Fourier transform convention, it specifically comes out to F(t) = sqrt(π) e^(-t^2/4).

@sigfpe Furthermore, the Fourier transform of f(x - 1) is e^(it) F(t), and the Fourier transform of f(x + 1) is symmetrically e^(-it) F(t). Combining these, we get that the Fourier transform of g(x) is G(t) = (e^(it) - e^(-it)) F(t) = sqrt(π) (e^(it) - e^(-it)) e^(-t^2/4).

@sigfpe The Fourier transform of g(x)/x, then, will be an integral of G(t) i, which is to say, an integral of i sqrt(π) (e^(it) - e^(-it)) e^(-t^2/4).

Which integral? Well, our g(x)/x has been chosen to be continuous even at 0, so its Fourier transform has to decay to zero at infinity.
