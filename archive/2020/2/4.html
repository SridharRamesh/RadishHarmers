"Margin of error" on polls isn't what you think. Generally, it just means 98% divided by the square root of the number of people polled. There's a reason statisticians care about this calculation, but frankly, it's misleading terminology and not at all easy to interpret or apply.


Timestamp: 2020-2-4 0:53:37
Id: 1224496194979844096

Pedantically, if you see a reported margin of error where this isn't the case, it's because sometimes polls make some people's votes count more than others ("raking", reweighting votes to counter demographic proportions mismatch between their samples and the population at large).


Timestamp: 2020-2-4 1:1:5
Id: 1224498074606882816

In this case the "margin of error" becomes 98% times the square root of the sum of the squared proportion of the poll result each individual is responsible for. (In the equal weight case, this reduces to the formula from before.)

This is much less common, and no more magical.


Timestamp: 2020-2-4 1:1:15
Id: 1224498117480996864

The sole thing this "margin of error" describes is an average level of reality mismatch for polls of such size to hit due to random samples happening, unluckily, by pure coincidence, to be unrepresentative.
